\chapter{The synthetic fleet: SynCFM56}
\label{ch:fleet}

\begin{quote}\itshape
This chapter in brief: one hundred virtual engines live full lives --- fouling, washes, wear,
the occasional bird strike and sensor drift --- while the generator records both what an
airline would see and the hidden truth. Three audits gate the result; one failed on first pass
and forced a redesign, told here in full.
\end{quote}

\keyidea{Every degradation mechanism is a closed-form function of flight cycle stored per-mechanism, so the fleet carries not just \emph{how} degraded each engine is but \emph{by what} --- and a deliberately weak classifier must \emph{fail} the difficulty gate, or the benchmark is too easy.}


\Cref{ch:data} established what the data \emph{is} and where it comes from; this chapter
documents phase F2: the generator that turns the F1 twin into a 100-engine
run-to-failure fleet with complete ground truth, the audits that gate it (one of which failed
on first pass and forced a design change --- documented in full in \cref{sec:audits}), and the
declaration of milestone H2.

\section{Generator architecture: linearize, then audit}
\label{sec:fleet-arch}

A fleet of 100 engines flying $\sim$16\,000 cycles each, two snapshots per flight, would
require over three million full pyCycle solves per fleet realization --- unusable for
iteration. Instead, the generator exploits exactly what F1 built: every snapshot is produced by
the linearization
\begin{equation}
  z(u, \mathbf{x}) \;=\; z_0(u)\,\bigl(1 + \mathbf{H}(u)\,\mathbf{x}/100\bigr),
  \label{eq:generator}
\end{equation}
where $z_0(u)$ is the healthy baseline and $\mathbf{H}(u)$ the influence coefficient matrix,
both linearly interpolated between the ICM grid points that bracket the sampled operating
condition (takeoff snapshots: in $\Delta T_{\mathrm{ISA}}$; cruise snapshots: in commanded N1).
N1 itself carries zero deviation by construction --- it is the held power-setting parameter,
not a health channel. The whole fleet generates in 40 seconds on all cores of the Apple M5 reference machine (one worker process
per engine, norm N1), with per-engine deterministic random seeds so the output is reproducible
regardless of process scheduling.

Two properties of this choice must be kept distinct. First, the dataset is
\emph{self-consistent by construction}: the recorded ground truth $\mathbf{x}$ is exactly the
state that generated the measurements, so estimator evaluations against truth are exact
regardless of any linearization error. Second, \emph{fidelity to the full nonlinear physics} is
an empirical question --- which is why the first gate audit re-solves full pyCycle at states
sampled from the generated fleet (\cref{sec:audit-nonlin}).

\section{Ground-truth trajectories}
\label{sec:trajectories}

Each engine draws a degradation ``personality'' from the fault catalog
(\code{conf/fault_catalog.yaml}, the versioned contract): a log-normal severity multiplier per
mechanism, a wash schedule, Poisson-arriving foreign-object damage, optional sensor drifts, and
---in roughly half the fleet--- one \emph{acute fault episode} (\cref{sec:audit-difficulty}
explains why these exist). The mechanisms follow the physical profiles of
\cref{tab:degradation}. Each is a closed-form function of flight cycle $n$, scaled by a
per-engine severity multiplier $m$ (drawn log-normal, so the fleet spans easy and hard
degraders) and applied to the component's efficiency/flow entries of $\mathbf{x}(n)$.

\subsection{The degradation models, formally}
\label{sec:degradation-models}

Let $n$ index flight cycles and $m$ be the engine's severity multiplier for the mechanism.
Each mechanism contributes to the health vector as follows (the exact forms in
\code{trajectories.py}, so the report and the generator cannot diverge --- norm N4).

\paragraph{Fouling --- saturating exponential with an exact wash sawtooth.} Dirt
accumulates toward an asymptote with time constant $\tau$ (cycles), and each compressor
wash instantaneously removes a fraction $r_k$ of the \emph{current} deposit. Working with a
normalized level $s(n) \in [0,1]$ (the per-parameter magnitude is applied afterwards), the
trajectory is computed \emph{segment-wise} between washes at cycles $w_1 < w_2 < \cdots$:
\begin{equation}
  s(n) = 1 + \bigl(s_k^{+} - 1\bigr)\, e^{-(n - w_k)/\tau}
  \quad\text{for } n \in [w_k, w_{k+1}), \qquad
  s_k^{+} = (1 - r_k)\, s(w_k^{-}),
  \label{eq:fouling}
\end{equation}
where $s(w_k^{-})$ is the level just before the wash and $s_k^{+}$ just after. The
contribution to a component's parameter is $m\,a\,s(n)$ with $a$ the asymptotic magnitude
(e.g.\ $\Delta\Gamma_{\text{HPC}} = -4\,\%$). The subtlety this equation encodes is that
regrowth always aims at the \emph{same} asymptote: a tempting shortcut --- permanently
subtracting the removed amount --- would make the trajectory approach
$\text{asymptote} - \text{removed}$ and under-predict late-life fouling (the second
implementation detail below).

\paragraph{Erosion --- slow linear.} Abrasive wear removes material at a constant rate
$\rho$ (percent per kilocycle):
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\eta(n) = m\,\rho\,\frac{n}{1000}.
  \label{eq:erosion}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Tip-clearance opening --- bilinear.} Clearances open fast during an initial
break-in of $n_b$ cycles, then continue at a slower steady rate $\rho_\ell$:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\eta(n) = m\Bigl[\, c\, \min\!\bigl(\tfrac{n}{n_b}, 1\bigr)
  \;+\; \rho_\ell\, \frac{\max(n - n_b,\, 0)}{1000} \,\Bigr],
  \label{eq:bilinear}
\end{equation}
with $c$ the break-in magnitude --- the ``fast initially, then slow'' profile of
\cref{tab:degradation}.

\paragraph{Hot-section deterioration --- linearly accelerating.} Creep and oxidation
compound, so the linear rate is multiplied by an accelerating factor with exponent $p$:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\eta(n) = m\,\rho\,\frac{n}{1000}\,
  \Bigl[\,1 + \bigl(\tfrac{n}{10^4}\bigr)^{p}\,\Bigr],
  \label{eq:hotsection}
\end{equation}
which produces the late-life upturn in EGT margin that ends most engines' lives.

\paragraph{Foreign-object damage --- Poisson step.} Impacts arrive as a Poisson process;
each deposits a permanent efficiency step $\Delta\eta_{\text{step}}$ on the struck
component from its arrival cycle onward (a Heaviside step). This is the acute counterpart
to the smooth chronic mechanisms.

\paragraph{Acute fault episode --- ramp then hold.} An acute fault ramps a single health
parameter over $n_r$ cycles from onset $n_0$, then holds:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta x_j(n) = M\,\operatorname{clip}\!\Bigl(\tfrac{n - n_0}{n_r},\, 0,\, 1\Bigr),
  \label{eq:acute}
\end{equation}
with magnitude $M$ --- these are the episodes the difficulty gate
(\cref{sec:audit-difficulty}) requires, and up to three may occur per engine in v1.1.

The total health state is the sum of all active mechanisms, $\mathbf{x}(n) = \sum_{\text{mech}}
\mathbf{x}^{\text{mech}}(n)$, and each mechanism's contribution is stored separately (the
composability property below) so any later analysis can ask not just how degraded an engine
is but \emph{by what}.

One implementation detail beyond the equations is worth recording. \textbf{Composability of
truth}: the generator stores not only the total $\mathbf{x}(n)$ but each mechanism's separate
contribution (\cref{eq:fouling,eq:erosion,eq:bilinear,eq:hotsection,eq:acute} evaluated
independently), so any later analysis can ask not just ``how degraded'' but ``degraded by
\emph{what}'' --- the property the mechanism-level tasks of \cref{ch:overcoming} will exploit.

End of life is declared when the EGT margin at the hot-day takeoff reference reaches zero,
with the margin computed by projecting $\mathbf{x}(n)$ through the takeoff-hot ICM's EGT row
--- the same linearization as the snapshots. New-engine margins are drawn per engine
($85\pm6$\,\si{\degreeCelsius}). \Cref{fig:egtm} shows the resulting fleet behavior: the
canonical fast-early / slow-late erosion with wash sawtooth, and lives spanning
roughly 10\,000--20\,700 cycles (v1.1 exact figures in \cref{tab:fleet}).

\section{Snapshots and the measurement model}
\label{sec:snapshot-model}

Each flight cycle emits two reports, mimicking airline ACARS practice: a takeoff snapshot (sea
level, ambient offset drawn from a climate distribution) and a cruise snapshot
(FL350/M0.78, commanded N1 drawn per flight). Truth channels pass through the sensor model of
the catalog: per-channel Gaussian noise (absolute or percentage $\sigma$), quantization to the
recording resolution, additive drift ramps on the affected engines (37 of 100 --- matching the
catalog's per-sensor probabilities summing to 0.36), and missing data as isolated losses plus
ACARS outage bursts ($\approx$4\,\% of snapshots). Every row carries, alongside the measured values:
the true channel values, the full ground-truth health vector, the EGT margin, the remaining
useful life, the isolation label, and drift flags. \Cref{tab:fleet} summarizes the frozen
dataset.

\section{Gate audits --- including the one that failed}
\label{sec:audits}

A synthetic dataset is only as trustworthy as the checks it survives. Three audits gate this
one, and the discipline is that they are allowed to fail: the difficulty audit did, on the
first design, and forced the redesign told below. Each audit asks a different question ---
is the physics faithful, is the benchmark hard enough, is the behaviour realistic?

\subsection{Nonlinearity of the generator}
\label{sec:audit-nonlin}

Eighty health states were sampled from the generated fleet (stratified by life fraction,
half from the last 30\,\% of life where deviations are largest) and re-solved with full
pyCycle at the exact ICM grid conditions, so the comparison isolates nonlinearity from
interpolation. \Cref{tab:nonlin} reports the per-channel error of \cref{eq:generator} against
the full solution, next to the sensor noise it must be judged against: medians of
0.01--0.33\,\%, tails (P95) at or below 1\,\%, concentrated at end of life. Combined with the
self-consistency property of \cref{sec:fleet-arch}, the linearization is adequate: the residual
infidelity to full physics is at the noise floor, identical for both EHM pipelines, and
irrelevant to truth-referenced scoring.

\subsection{Difficulty: the audit that failed, and what it taught}
\label{sec:audit-difficulty}

The difficulty gate is designed to prevent a trivially easy dataset from flattering the AI: a
deliberately weak classifier (multinomial logistic regression on the 16 raw measured channels)
must \emph{not} solve the isolation task; the pre-set threshold is 60\,\% accuracy.

\textbf{First pass: failed.} With the original labeling --- the dominant \emph{chronic}
mechanism per snapshot --- the trivial classifier scored \textbf{81.6\,\%} (majority-class
baseline 54.6\,\%). Root-cause analysis: chronic mechanisms co-evolve with age in \emph{every}
engine (fouling saturates early, hot-section deterioration accumulates late), so the chronic
label is largely an \emph{age proxy}, and raw absolute channel levels encode age directly. The
task the label defined was not fault isolation at all. Two responses were possible: relax the
threshold (cosmetic) or fix the design (structural). The structural fix was taken.

\textbf{Design change: acute fault episodes.} What hypothesis H2 actually tests is the
isolation of \emph{localized faults with overlapping signatures} --- the regime where the ICM
observability analysis proved cockpit sensors nearly blind (minimum signature angle
1.3$^{\circ}$, \cref{tab:confusable}). The catalog therefore gained discrete
\emph{single-parameter} fault episodes: a fast ramp (50--500 cycles) to a sustained magnitude
on one health parameter, drawn from six targets that deliberately include the confusable pairs
($\eta_{\mathrm{HPC}}$ vs.\ $\eta_{\mathrm{HPT}}$, $\eta_{\mathrm{HPT}}$ vs.\
$\Gamma_{\mathrm{HPT}}$), injected in $\sim$50\,\% of engines at a random point of their lives.
The gated task became: classify each snapshot as \emph{no acute fault} or by the faulted
parameter --- a 7-class isolation problem.

\textbf{The audit also caught a generator bug.} The first acute implementation drew the episode
onset as a fraction of the \emph{maximum simulation horizon} (30\,000 cycles) rather than of
the engine's actual life ($\sim$16\,000): most episodes landed beyond end of life and only
15/100 engines showed one. The fix computes a chronic-only provisional life first, then places
the onset as a fraction of it (two-pass generation); the v1.0 fleet then carried 49/100 acute engines
with all six target parameters represented (v1.1 extends this further,
\cref{sec:fleet-v11}).

\textbf{Second pass: passed.} On the corrected fleet the trivial classifier achieves
\textbf{54.0\,\%} accuracy (macro-F1 0.30) on the gated isolation task --- under the 60\,\%
threshold, exactly as the signature-angle physics predicts (\cref{tab:difficulty-audit}). The
chronic-mechanism label is retained in the dataset and reported as a secondary,
explicitly-not-gated metric (81.6\,\% accuracy --- high, expected, and uninformative about
isolation ability). The episode is recorded here in full because it is evidence the gates are
real: a pre-registered criterion failed, the failure was diagnosed to a design flaw rather than
argued away, and the fix strengthened the benchmark.

\subsection{Realism}
\label{sec:audit-realism}

Quantitative checks of macroscopic behavior, all in \cref{tab:fleet}: the wash sawtooth is
visible (mean exhaust-gas-temperature-margin (EGTM) recovery $+2.8$\,\si{\degreeCelsius}, positive at 99.75\,\% of the 400
sampled wash events); end-of-life deviations carry the physical deterioration signature in
100\,\% of engines ($\Delta$EGT $+68$\,K, $\Delta W_f$ $+5.7$\,\%); the measured-noise
residual on drift-free engines reproduces the catalog $\sigma$ (2.51 vs.\ 2.50\,K); lives
center on 15\,400 cycles with a realistic severe-degrader tail and no censored engines.

\subsection{Version 1.1: more episodes, and the gate bites again}
\label{sec:fleet-v11}

The engine-level sample starvation diagnosed in \cref{ch:ai} (v1.0 carried at most one acute
episode per engine) was fixed in \textbf{SynCFM56 v1.1}: a chain of conditional episode
probabilities gives up to three non-overlapping episodes per engine (minimum gap 2\,500
cycles, onsets as fractions of actual life). The result: 79 engines with 114 episodes,
every fault class with $\geq 9$ training instances, and 23 evaluable test episodes instead
of ten.

Re-auditing v1.1 produced a second demonstration that the difficulty gate is real: with the
original fault magnitudes, the trivial classifier now scored \textbf{62.2\,\%} --- more
episodes feed the trivial classifier too, and the gate (60\,\%) failed again. The response
was again physical rather than cosmetic: episode magnitudes were scaled by 0.75 (subtler
faults, closer to the detectability edge), bringing the trivial classifier to
\textbf{58.1\,\%} --- pass. The nonlinearity audit was re-run on v1.1 with unchanged
conclusions (medians 0.14--0.34\,\%, P95 $\leq$ 1\,\%).

\section{Milestone H2: declared closed}
\label{sec:h2}

All gate criteria are met: realism and difficulty audits passed (the latter after the
documented design change), nonlinearity quantified at the noise floor, split-by-engine
leakage checks automated in CI, and the dataset datasheet written
(\code{docs/syncfm56-datasheet.md}). SynCFM56 v1.1 is the frozen evaluation dataset (v1.0 history retained above): regeneration is deterministic
from the catalog and seed, so the dataset is fully specified by the repository state. Known
limitations, declared for downstream chapters: linearized generation (audited,
\cref{tab:nonlin}); two fixed snapshot conditions with ambient/power scatter rather than a
full mission mix; chronic labels are age-correlated by nature; and at most three acute episodes per engine (v1.1).
