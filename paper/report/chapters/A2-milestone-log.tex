\section*{What a gate is, and why a dated log matters}

This study was organized as a sequence of \emph{gates}: pre-declared, quantitative
pass/fail criteria that each phase had to meet before the next could begin. A gate is not a
progress marker --- it is a commitment made \emph{before} seeing the outcome. ``The synthetic
fleet passes only if a trivial classifier scores below 60\,\%'' is a gate; ``the fleet looked
realistic to us'' is not. Gates are what keep a single-team study honest: when a gate fails
(and several did --- the difficulty gate twice, a hybrid hypothesis twice), the failure is
recorded and the design is fixed physically, never by quietly relaxing the threshold.

The log below is the dated record of every gate outcome, kept \emph{as written at the time}.
That last property is the whole value of the appendix. Numbers in early entries are sometimes
superseded by later, better measurements --- the entries are deliberately \emph{not} updated,
because a log that is edited after the fact certifies nothing. Where a number has been
superseded, the body chapters carry the current value and say so; the entry here shows what
was known, and claimed, on the day. Together with the pre-registration tags (frozen protocol
documents referenced throughout, \cref{sec:safeguards}), this gives a reader the means to verify
that hypotheses, thresholds, and budgets were fixed before results were looked at --- the
same audit trail a regulator or a skeptical reviewer would ask for.

Each entry is written to be readable on its own: what was gated, what the evidence was, and
where in the body the full account lives.
